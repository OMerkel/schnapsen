# Schnapsen (Two Players) - Traditional Rules

Schnapsen is a trick-taking card game for two players. The goal in each hand is to reach **66 card points or more** by winning tricks and announcing marriages (a suited pair of **King + Ober**).

## 1. Cards and Card Values

Use a 20-card deck (German or equivalent):

- Ace (Ass) = 11 points
- Ten (Zehn) = 10 points
- King (Koenig) = 4 points
- Ober = 3 points
- Unter = 2 points

Total trick-card value in the deck: 120 points.

### Variant: 24 Cards (Including 9s)

In this variant, a **9** is added in each suit (24 cards total).

- The 9 is worth **0 points**.
- Rank order becomes (high to low): **Ace, Ten, King, Ober, Unter, 9**.
- The target remains **66 points**.

Even in the 24-card variant, the total value of counting trick cards remains 120 points.

## 2. Objective

Win the hand by being the first player to reach at least **66 points** (from tricks and marriages).

## 3. Deal and Trump

1. Determine the dealer randomly.
2. Deal 5 cards to each player.
3. Place the remaining cards face down as the talon (stock).
4. Turn the top talon card face up; its suit is trump.
5. The non-dealer leads the first trick.

## 4. How Tricks Work

### While the talon is still open

- The leader may play any card.
- The second player may play any card (no suit-follow obligation).
- A trick is won by the higher card of the led suit, unless a trump is played; any trump beats any non-trump.
- If both cards are trumps, higher trump rank wins.

Card rank order in the 20-card deck (high to low): **Ace, Ten, King, Ober, Unter**.

After each trick:

1. The trick winner takes the trick and leads next.
2. The trick winner draws first from the talon.
3. The other player draws next.

### When the talon is exhausted or closed

Strict play rules apply:

1. You must follow suit if possible.
2. If following suit, you must head the trick if you can.
3. If you cannot follow suit, you must play a trump if possible.
4. If neither is possible, you may play any card.

## 5. Marriage (King + Ober)

A Marriage (a matching pair of King and Ober in the same suit) can only be announced by the active leader.
So the announcement is valid only when you are currently on lead; otherwise you must wait until you lead again.

- A Marriage is announced by leading either the King or the Ober of a suit while holding the matching partner card. You show both cards, but play only one.
- Marriage in a non-trump suit is worth **20 points**.
- A trump Marriage is worth **40 points**.

Important:

- Marriage points count only if the announcing player wins at least one trick in that hand.
- If the player already has a trick, add marriage points immediately.
- If not, record them and add them when that player wins their first trick.

## 6. Closing the Talon (Optional Tactical Action)

If you are on lead and the talon is still open, you may close it before leading a card.

Effects of closing:

- No more cards are drawn from the talon.
- From then on, strict play rules (follow suit, head trick, trump if needed) apply immediately.
- The closer commits to reaching 66 with the current hands; if they fail, they lose the hand.

## 7. Ending the Hand

A hand ends immediately when a player reaches **66 or more points** and declares it.

If neither player declares 66 before cards run out:

- Play all remaining cards.
- Winner of the final trick receives a **10-point last-trick bonus**.
- Higher total wins the hand.

## 8. Hand Scoring (Game Points)

Game points are also called "Bummerl".

Traditional scoring per hand:

- Winner gets **1 game point** if loser has 33 or more card points.
- Winner gets **2 game points** if loser has 0-32 card points.
- Winner gets **3 game points** if loser won no trick.

If a player closes the talon and fails to reach 66, that player loses; opponent scores according to the same 1/2/3 scale.

## 9. Match Scoring

Several hands form a match.

- Standard: first to **7 game points** (that is, 7 Bummerl) wins the match.

You "give Bummerl" to the loser. In Austria, having a Bummerl can also mean bad luck or losing in colloquial speech.

---

This rules text follows common traditional two-player Schnapsen with Marriage (King-Ober pairs) and 20 or 24 cards.
